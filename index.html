Key Insights

Patterns of success and failure identified across global newborn screening implementations

What Works: 5 Critical Success Factors

1

Comprehensive Ethical Framework

Establish clear guidelines for consent, incidental findings, and data governance before launch

2

Phased Implementation Through Pilots

2-3 year pilot programs essential before population-wide rollout

3

Healthcare Provider Engagement

Early investment in obstetrician and pediatrician education

4

Adequate Genetic Counseling Capacity

Ratio of 1:3000 counselors to births predicts parental satisfaction

5

Integration with Existing Screening

Build upon existing infrastructure for faster, cost-effective implementation

What Fails: 5 Common Failure Modes

1

Incidental Findings Overwhelm

15-30% of resources consumed by uncertain findings unrelated to targets

2

Premature Population Rollout

Scaling before completing pilot evaluation leads to operational failures

3

Discordant Results Create Confusion

Without reconciliation protocols, conflicting results cause clinical uncertainty

4

Workforce Bottlenecks

Underestimating genetic counseling and bioinformatics needs

5

Inequitable Access

Academic center models create socioeconomic disparities

Critical Finding

"The current evidence base does not support routine population-wide WGS for newborn screening. Programs should prioritize building evidence through well-designed research while preparing infrastructure for future implementation when evidence thresholds are met."

— GENEQ Global Analysis, January 2026
