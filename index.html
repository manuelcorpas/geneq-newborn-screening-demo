Phased Implementation is Non-Negotiable

Every successful large-scale programme began with a 2-3 year pilot. Programmes skipping this phase report 3x higher protocol modification rates post-launch.

Evidence:
  • UK Generation Study: 4+ years co-design before launch
  • Australia BabyScreen+: 3-year pilot generating evidence
  • Netherlands SONNET: 2-year pilot with 50 conditions
2

Workforce is the Hidden Bottleneck

Programmes consistently underestimate genetic counseling needs. 40-60% knowledge gaps exist among providers before training.

Critical Ratios:
  • Genetic counselors: Minimum 1:3,000 screened births
  • Wait times >4 weeks = compromised clinical utility
  • Provider education must precede programme launch
3

Cost-Effectiveness Remains Unproven

Current WGS costs 1,780x more than traditional screening. ROI depends on early diagnosis preventing costly late interventions.

Cost Reality:
  • WGS per baby: ¬£1,050 (UK current)
  • Traditional heel-prick: ¬£0.59
  • Future projection: $100/genome at scale
  • UK national rollout budget: ¬£650M
4

Equity Must Be Built In, Not Retrofitted

Programmes implemented through academic centers create socioeconomic enrollment bias. Rural and minority populations face systematic access barriers.

Equity Gaps:
  • Genomic databases underrepresent non-European populations
  • Cultural adaptation of consent processes often lacking
  • Follow-up care access varies by geography/income

Risk Analysis: Common Failure Modes

Learn from programmes that struggled. These patterns appear repeatedly across failed or paused implementations.

1

Incidental Findings Overwhelm

WGS programmes without clear secondary findings policies become paralyzed by variant interpretation workload.

Impact:

15-30% of resources consumed by managing uncertain findings unrelated to target conditions

Prevention:

Establish explicit policies on reportable secondary findings categories before launch

2

Premature Population Rollout

Programmes scaling before pilot evaluation face operational failures, PR challenges, and quality concerns.

Impact:

Multiple programmes have scaled back or paused after premature expansion

Prevention:

Define specific evidence thresholds for scale-up; maintain pilot until met

3

Discordant Results Confusion

When genomic screening conflicts with conventional screening, programmes without reconciliation protocols face clinical uncertainty.

Impact:

BabySeq research documents significant discordance requiring careful management

Prevention:

Design integrated workflows with clear protocols for result discordance

4

Workforce Bottlenecks

Programmes frequently underestimate genetic counseling, bioinformatics, and clinical genetics requirements.

Impact:

Wait times exceeding 4 weeks compromise clinical utility

Prevention:

Conduct workforce modeling before launch; invest in training pipelines

5

Inequitable Access

Programmes implemented through academic centers or requiring parental initiative create socioeconomic disparities.

Impact:
  • ‚Ä¢ Research programmes report enrollment bias toward higher socioeconomic populations
  • ‚Ä¢ Rural populations in US, Canada, Australia face systematic barriers
  • ‚Ä¢ Non-European populations underrepresented in reference databases
Prevention:
  • ‚Ä¢ Design universal access mechanisms from inception
  • ‚Ä¢ Monitor demographic representation continuously
  • ‚Ä¢ Require equity impact assessments in programme design
